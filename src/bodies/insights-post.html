Why Vietnamese coconut is mispriced — and what closes the gap

Spot pricing rewards volume, not value. We walk the chain from grove to L/C desk to find where the margin actually hides.

LT
Lê Minh TríHead of Trade Desk
Jun 12, 2026·8 min read
Bến Tre to port — one controlled chain

Walk into any spot market for Vietnamese coconut and you will hear one number: price per nut. Walk the chain backwards — to the L/C desk, the factory floor, the grove — and that number explains almost nothing about where value is made, or lost.

Vietnam is among the world's largest coconut producers, yet the fruit leaves the country priced as a raw commodity. The gap between that price and the value a finished ingredient commands abroad is not a market failure. It is a structuring failure — and structuring is something a balance sheet can fix.

The margin hides between the links

Every hand-off in the chain — collector to trader, trader to processor, processor to exporter — is a place where risk is repriced and a margin is taken. Each intermediary is paid for absorbing uncertainty for a few days. Stack enough of them and the grower is left with the thinnest slice of a number the end buyer pays in full.

Owning the chain removes those internal tolls. But ownership alone is not enough; the capital behind it is what lets you hold a price when the season turns.

We don't sell coconut. We sell the certainty that it arrives — at spec, on time, at the price agreed.

Certainty is the product

A buyer in Rotterdam or Busan is not, ultimately, buying desiccated coconut. They are buying the assurance that a contracted volume, at a contracted specification, arrives at a contracted price — regardless of what the weather did in Bến Tre that quarter. Price that assurance correctly and the commodity discount disappears.

So what actually closes the gap between the nut and its finished value? Four moves, none of them exotic:

  • 01Supply-zone locking — contracted groves, not spot purchases.
  • 02Trade finance that absorbs price volatility instead of passing it down the chain.
  • 03One counterparty for sourcing, processing, documents and finance.
  • 04Traceability that turns a marketing claim into an auditable record.
