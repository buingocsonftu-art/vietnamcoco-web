To use coco coir bricks, submerge one compressed 5 kg block in roughly 20–25 litres of water and soak for 15–30 minutes. The brick expands to approximately 70–85 litres of loose, ready-to-use growing media. After hydration, break apart any remaining clumps, fluff the coir, and rinse if the electrical conductivity (EC) exceeds 0.5 mS/cm. This article walks through every step — from hydration to mixing ratios to storage — so you get consistent results the first time.

## What Is a Coco Coir Brick?

A coco coir brick is a compressed block of coconut fibre and coco peat harvested from the husk of mature coconuts. During production at a facility like ours in Vietnam, raw coconut husks are washed, dried, screened, and then mechanically compressed into dense rectangular blocks — typically weighing 1 kg, 5 kg, or 650 g depending on the supplier. Compression reduces shipping volume by roughly 85–90 percent, making coir bricks far cheaper to transport than loose media. When you add water, the fibres rehydrate and expand dramatically. The result is a lightweight, pH-stable medium (typically pH 5.8–6.8) that holds up to eight times its dry weight in water. For full technical data on fibre grade, moisture content, and EC levels, see our [coir and coco peat specifications](/insights/coir-and-coco-peat-specifications) page.

## How to Use Coco Coir Bricks: Step-by-Step Hydration

**What you need:** a large container or wheelbarrow (at least 60 litres), clean water, and a shovel or garden fork.

### Step 1 — Measure the water

Place a 5 kg coir brick in your container. Add 20–25 litres of room-temperature water. Some growers prefer a 1:4 brick-to-water ratio (by volume); others use a 1:5 ratio for a looser, more moisture-retentive final mix. Start with 20 litres and add more if dry pockets remain after soaking.

### Step 2 — Soak for 15 to 30 minutes

Let the brick sit undisturbed. Thin bricks (1 kg or 650 g) fully hydrate in 10–15 minutes. A dense 5 kg block typically needs 20–30 minutes. You will see the block split and swell visibly during this period.

### Step 3 — Break apart and fluff

Once the brick has absorbed most of the water, use your hands or a garden fork to break apart remaining compressed chunks. Work through the material until the texture is uniform — resembling loose, damp potting soil with visible short fibres. If the coir feels soupy, drain excess water through the container's holes or tip it out.

### Step 4 — Check and adjust moisture

Squeeze a handful. It should release only a few drops. If water streams out, spread the coir on a tarp for 30–60 minutes to drain. If it crumbles without clumping, mist lightly and mix again.

## Seed Starting Mix vs. Container Mix: The Right Ratios

The coir-to-perlite ratio determines drainage and aeration. Here is what works for two common applications:

| Application | Coir : Perlite : Other | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Seed starting** | 60 : 40 (by volume) | Seeds need high moisture retention and minimal compaction. Fine coco peat works best here — sieve out long fibres. |
| **Container growing** | 70 : 20 : 10 compost (by volume) | Containers need more structure. Adding 10–20 percent perlite prevents waterlogging, while a small compost fraction supplies starter nutrients. |

These ratios are starting points. Heavy-feeding crops (tomatoes, peppers) benefit from a richer compost share. Succulents and cacti tolerate up to 50 percent perlite. ## Why You Should Rinse and Buffer Low-EC Coir

Not all coir bricks are ready to plant into straight from the bag. Two quality concerns matter:

**Electrical conductivity (EC).** Coir processed near coastal areas or with seawater-contacted husks may carry residual salts. An EC above 0.5 mS/cm (measured in a 1:1.5 coir-to-water slurry) can stunt seedlings and sensitive crops. To rinse: spread the hydrated coir, flush with clean water (roughly equal volume to the coir), let drain, and test again. Repeat until EC drops below your target.

**Cation exchange capacity (CEC).** Raw coir fibre naturally holds potassium (K) and sodium (Na) on its exchange sites. If you do not buffer, those ions will displace calcium and magnesium in your nutrient solution over the first weeks, causing deficiency symptoms. Buffering is straightforward: soak rinsed coir in a calcium nitrate solution (about 1–2 g per litre) for 8–12 hours, then drain. This pre-loads exchange sites with calcium and stabilises nutrient uptake from day one.

Many Vietnamese processors — including us — pre-wash and pre-buffer coir to EC levels below 0.5 mS/cm before compression. If your supplier's [coir and coco peat specifications](/insights/coir-and-coco-peat-specifications) show buffered, low-EC product, you can skip this step and hydrate directly.

## How to Store Unused Coco Coir Brick

An unused, still-compressed brick stores well for 12–18 months if you keep it dry and off the ground. Moisture in the packaging invites mould and pre-hydration, both of which ruin shelf life.

**Practical storage tips:**

- Keep bricks in their original shrink-wrap or re-seal in heavy-duty plastic bags.
- Store in a covered, ventilated shed — not in direct sun (UV degrades the outer fibres) and not in a damp basement.
- If you hydrated more coir than you need, spread leftover coir in a thin layer (5–10 cm) and let it air-dry to under 20 percent moisture before bagging it again. Fully dry coir resists mould; damp coir does not.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much water does a 5 kg coco coir brick need?

A standard 5 kg compressed coir brick requires approximately 20–25 litres of water to fully hydrate. The final expanded volume lands between 70 and 85 litres, depending on the fibre grade and compression pressure used during manufacturing.

### Can I reuse coco coir after a growing season?

Yes. Spent coir can be reconditioned by rinsing to remove old nutrient salts, re-buffering with a calcium nitrate soak, and remixing with fresh perlite (add 20–30 percent new perlite to restore aeration). Coir fibre degrades slowly — you can typically reuse it for two to three seasons before structure breaks down noticeably.

### Is coco coir pH neutral?

Coir generally runs slightly acidic to neutral, with a pH range of 5.8 to 6.8 depending on the wash and buffer process. This sits in the sweet spot for most vegetables and ornamentals. If your water source is alkaline (above pH 7.5), a small addition of peat or pH-adjusted nutrient solution keeps the root zone in range.

### Do I need to add nutrients to coco coir?

Yes. Coir contains very little plant-available nitrogen, phosphorus, or potassium. Treat it as an inert medium and provide a complete hydroponic or organic nutrient programme from the first watering. A light compost blend (10 percent by volume) supplies trace biology and slow-release nutrients in container mixes.

### What is the difference between coco coir bricks and loose coco coir?

Only the form differs. Bricks are compressed for shipping efficiency; loose coir is the same material already hydrated and broken down. One 5 kg brick produces the same 70–85 litres you would buy loose — but at a fraction of the freight cost, which is why most commercial growers order bricks.
