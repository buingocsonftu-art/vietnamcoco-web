**Short answer:** for most greenhouse and hydroponic crops, specify washed and buffered coco peat at EC ≤ 0.5 mS/cm (≤ 0.3 for sensitive crops), pH 5.5 to 6.8, low sodium and chloride, a stated pith-to-chip ratio for your crop, and an expansion volume per block. Raw, unwashed coir is cheaper and will cost you the first crop. Here is why each number matters.

Coco peat is sold by the container and judged by the root. A grower does not see the salt that came off the husk until three weeks in, when the seedlings sit still and the leaf tips burn. That is not bad media. It is unwashed, unbuffered [coir](/products/husk) bought on price. The specification is the difference, and most of it comes down to three numbers and one process.

## The forms, and which one your crop needs
- **Pith (coco peat):** the fine, sponge-like fraction. Holds water and air; the base of most potting and propagation mixes.
- **Chips and husk chips:** coarse pieces that hold air and drain fast. Mixed into pith to open up the media for crops that hate wet feet.
- **The ratio is the spec.** Strawberry, cucumber and rose growers buy a stated **pith-to-chip ratio**, commonly 70/30 or 80/20. A grow bag at 70/30 drains and aerates differently from one at 80/20. "Coco" without a ratio is not a specification.
- **Blocks, bags and grow bags:** a 5 kg block (about 30×30×12 cm) expands to roughly **70 to 85 litres** of media. Buy by **expansion volume and bulk density**, not weight alone, because that is what fills your benches.

## EC, pH, and why "washed" is not "buffered"
Two numbers and one process decide whether coir grows a crop or starves it.

**EC (electrical conductivity)** measures the salt the media will release. Coconut husks are soaked in lagoons that are often brackish, so raw coir carries high sodium, potassium and chloride. Low-EC coir sits at **EC ≤ 0.5 mS/cm**; high-EC raw coir runs **above 0.8**. That salt does not stay put. It leaches into your feed and throws off the recipe.

**Washing** rinses the loose salt out with fresh water over repeated cycles, which lowers EC. But washing alone is not the finish line.

**Buffering** is the step price-led buyers skip. Coir's cation-exchange sites hold sodium and potassium that washing will not remove, and they release later, exactly when your nutrient solution needs those sites for calcium and magnesium. Buffering with a **calcium-nitrate solution** displaces the sodium and potassium up front, so the media does not rob your feed mid-crop. **Washed and buffered** coir runs **EC ≤ 0.3 mS/cm, pH 5.8 to 6.5**, and that is what sensitive and long-cycle crops need.

So "low EC" tells you the salt was rinsed. "Buffered" tells you the chemistry was fixed. They are not the same line item, and the cheaper offer is usually the one washed but not buffered.

## The specifications that belong on the contract
| Parameter | Typical spec | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| EC | ≤ 0.5 mS/cm (≤ 0.3 washed + buffered) | Salt the media releases into your feed |
| pH | 5.5 to 6.8 | Nutrient availability; coir is naturally mildly acidic |
| Processing | Washed and buffered (calcium nitrate) | Removes Na and K from exchange sites, not just loose salt |
| Pith : chip ratio | Stated for the crop (e.g. 70/30, 80/20) | Water-to-air balance at the root |
| Expansion | ~70 to 85 L per 5 kg block | What actually fills the bench |
| Moisture, sand, fibre | Stated limits | Weight you pay freight on; grit that clogs drippers |
| Certification | RHP or equivalent where required | The horticulture substrate quality mark |

A contract that says "coco peat, low EC" and a price has not specified the product. It has left out buffering, the ratio and the expansion volume, the three things that decide the crop.

## Frequently asked questions

**What EC should coco peat be for growing?**
For most crops, EC ≤ 0.5 mS/cm; for sensitive or long-cycle crops, choose washed and buffered coir at EC ≤ 0.3. High-EC raw coir (above 0.8 mS/cm) carries salt that leaches into your feed.

**What is the difference between washed and buffered coco peat?**
Washing rinses loose salt out with fresh water, which lowers EC. Buffering then treats the coir with a calcium-nitrate solution to displace the sodium and potassium held on the cation-exchange sites, so they do not release mid-crop. Buffered coir is the safer choice for hydroponics and sensitive crops.

**What pith-to-chip ratio should I buy?**
It depends on the crop and system. Many strawberry, cucumber and rose growers use 70/30 or 80/20 pith-to-chip; more chips means more air and faster drainage. Specify the ratio rather than buying unspecified "coco".

**How much does a coco peat block expand?**
A 5 kg block (around 30×30×12 cm) expands to roughly 70 to 85 litres of media once hydrated. Buy by expansion volume and bulk density, not weight alone, because that is what fills your benches.

**Do I need RHP certification?**
RHP, or an equivalent substrate quality mark, is expected by many professional European growers and some retailers. Specify it where your market or crop insurance requires it, alongside the batch EC and pH report.
