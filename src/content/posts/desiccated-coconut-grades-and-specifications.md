Most desiccated coconut is bought on one word — "medium" — and a price. Then the container lands, the lab runs a certificate of analysis, and the argument starts: the fat is 58% not 65%, the sulphur trips an EU clean-label line, the microbial count is borderline. None of that is bad luck. It is an under-specified contract. Here is what a desiccated coconut spec sheet actually says, and which numbers belong in the contract before the first dollar moves.

## The three grades — and the one your product needs

**Fine** (particle below ~1.0 mm; passes a 1.70 mm sieve with very little held on 1.40 mm) disperses almost invisibly into batters and doughs. Reach for it in butter and sponge cakes, biscuits, chocolate fillings, coatings and energy-bar binding.

**Medium** (roughly 1.0–3.0 mm; passes 2.80 mm, little held on 2.00 mm) is the default grade of the global trade — visible coconut texture without dominating. When a recipe just says "desiccated coconut," it means medium.

**Coarse, thread, chips and macaroon** are the larger cuts, chosen for visible texture and topping; chips run 1.5–4.5 cm.

One thing buyers miss: fine grade absorbs moisture faster and is less stable in storage than coarse. Grade is a shelf-life and handling decision, not only a texture one.

## The specifications that actually decide quality

Grade is the cut. These are the numbers that decide whether the lot passes:

| Parameter | Typical spec | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Moisture | ≤ 3% (chips ≤ 2%) | Higher moisture cuts shelf life and raises microbial risk |
| Fat (oil), dry basis | High-fat 60–68%; low-fat / partially defatted 25–55% | Low fat can signal immature nuts, bleaching or adulteration |
| FFA (as lauric) | ≤ 0.2–0.3% | Rising free fatty acids mean rancidity is starting |
| SO2 (sulphur dioxide) | Free / ≤ 10 ppm for EU clean label; up to 50–100 ppm elsewhere | The spec that quietly fails EU shipments — see below |
| Microbiology | TPC < 5,000–10,000 CFU/g; Salmonella absent in 25 g | A food-safety gate, non-negotiable |
| Aflatoxin | B1 < 2 ppb, total < 4 ppb | A regulated contaminant |
| Shelf life | 12 months (some 18) | Match it to incoterm and transit time |

If your contract names a grade and a price but not these, you have not really specified the product — you have specified a dispute.

## Origin changes the spec — the part most guides skip

Two "high-fat medium" offers from two origins are not the same product. From real supplier spec sheets:

**Vietnam** (processors such as ACP – Asia Coconut Processing, Visimex, Artexport) typically runs fat around 62–66%, moisture ≤ 3%, and SO2 specified up to 100 ppm — with free-SO2 variants on request (ACP codes 6104 free / 6114 ≤ 50 / 6124 ≤ 100 ppm). Strong on price and proximity to the Mekong supply.

**Sri Lanka, Indonesia and the Philippines** (Bata Food, Jaindi/econutrena, Indo-Phil sources) tend to offer higher fat — oil ≥ 68%, up to ~72% — with SO2 free or below 10 ppm and a longer certification stack (BRC, IFS, ISO 22000, EU/USDA Organic, JAS, Kosher, Halal, Fair Trade), and shelf life often 18 months.

The lesson is not that one origin beats another. It is that the **SO2 ceiling and the certification set must match your destination market before you sign**. A 100 ppm sulphur spec that is fine for one buyer is a clean-label failure for another. Specify your destination's limit, not the supplier's default.

## From spec sheet to bankable contract

A specification only protects you if it is enforceable. Three things turn numbers on a PDF into a contract that holds:

- **A COA per lot, tied to the exact spec, with the test method named.** "≤ 3% moisture" means little without "measured by [method]."
- **Traceability to the processing batch**, so a failing parameter can be traced and fixed, not just rejected. Quality begins upstream.
- **One counterparty accountable for sourcing, processing, documentation and the finance behind it** — so a spec miss is one conversation, not four.
