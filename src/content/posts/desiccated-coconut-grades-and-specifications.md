**Short answer:** for most bakery and confectionery use, specify medium grade, high-fat (oil ≥ 60% dry basis), moisture ≤ 3%, SO₂ to your destination's clean-label limit, microbiology to a named standard, and a certificate of analysis per lot. The rest of this guide explains why each number belongs on the contract, and how origin changes it.

Most desiccated coconut is bought on one word, "medium", and a price. Then the container lands, the lab runs a certificate of analysis, and the argument starts: the fat is 58% not 65%, the sulphur trips an EU clean-label line, the microbial count is borderline. None of that is bad luck. It is an under-specified contract. Here is the [desiccated coconut](/products/desiccated-coconut) spec sheet read the way a trade desk reads it.

## The three grades, and the one your product needs
**Fine** (particle below ~1.0 mm; passes a 1.70 mm sieve, very little held on 1.40 mm) disperses almost invisibly into batters and doughs. Use it in butter and sponge cakes, biscuits, chocolate fillings, coatings, energy-bar binding.

**Medium** (roughly 1.0 to 3.0 mm; passes 2.80 mm, little held on 2.00 mm) is the default grade of the global trade, visible coconut texture without dominating. When a recipe just says "desiccated coconut", it means medium.

**Coarse, thread, chips and macaroon** are the larger cuts, for visible texture and topping; chips run 1.5 to 4.5 cm.

One thing buyers miss: fine grade absorbs moisture faster and is less stable in storage than coarse. Grade is a shelf-life and handling decision, not only a texture one.

## The specifications that actually decide quality
Grade is the cut. These are the numbers that decide whether the lot passes:

| Parameter | Typical spec | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Moisture | ≤ 3% (chips ≤ 2%) | Higher moisture cuts shelf life and raises microbial risk |
| Fat (oil), dry basis | High-fat 60 to 68%; low-fat / partially defatted 25 to 55% | Low fat can signal immature nuts, bleaching or adulteration |
| FFA (as lauric) | ≤ 0.2 to 0.3% | Rising free fatty acids mean rancidity is starting |
| SO₂ (sulphur dioxide) | Free or ≤ 10 ppm for EU clean label; up to 50 to 100 ppm elsewhere | The spec that quietly fails EU shipments (see below) |
| Microbiology | TPC < 5,000 to 10,000 CFU/g; Salmonella absent in 25 g | A food-safety gate, non-negotiable |
| Aflatoxin | B1 < 2 ppb, total < 4 ppb | A regulated contaminant |
| Shelf life | 12 months (some 18) | Match it to incoterm and transit time |

A contract that names a grade and a price but not these has not specified a product. It has specified a dispute. The fix is one line per parameter, each tied to a test method ("moisture ≤ 3% by [method]"), not just a number.

## Origin changes the spec, the part most guides skip
Two "high-fat medium" offers from two origins are not the same product. From real supplier spec sheets:

**Vietnam** (processors such as ACP, Visimex, Artexport) typically runs fat around 62 to 66%, moisture ≤ 3%, and SO₂ specified up to 100 ppm, with free-SO₂ variants on request (ACP codes 6104 free, 6114 ≤ 50, 6124 ≤ 100 ppm). Strong on price and proximity to the Mekong supply.

**Sri Lanka, Indonesia and the Philippines** tend to offer higher fat, oil ≥ 68% and up to ~72%, with SO₂ free or below 10 ppm and a longer certification stack (BRC, IFS, ISO 22000, EU/USDA Organic, JAS, Kosher, Halal, Fair Trade), shelf life often 18 months.

The lesson is not that one origin beats another. It is that the SO₂ ceiling and the certification set must match your destination market before you sign. A 100 ppm sulphur spec that is fine for one buyer is a clean-label failure for another. Specify your destination's limit, not the supplier's default.

## From spec sheet to a contract that holds
A specification only protects you if it is enforceable. Three things turn numbers on a PDF into a [bankable contract](/trade-finance):

- **A COA per lot, tied to the exact spec, with the test method named.** "≤ 3% moisture" means little without "measured by [method]".
- **[Traceability to the processing batch](/sustainability),** so a failing parameter can be traced and fixed, not just rejected. Quality begins upstream.
- **One counterparty accountable** for sourcing, processing, documentation and the finance behind it, so a spec miss is one conversation, not four.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is the difference between fine and medium desiccated coconut?**
Fine is below ~1.0 mm and disperses invisibly into batters; medium is 1.0 to 3.0 mm and leaves a visible coconut texture. Medium is the default global trade grade; fine is for baked goods where you do not want visible flakes.

**Why does SO₂ (sulphur dioxide) matter for EU shipments?**
Many origins specify sulphur dioxide up to 100 ppm as a preservative, but EU clean-label programmes and several retailers require free SO₂ or ≤ 10 ppm. A spec that passes in one market can be rejected at an EU port, so the SO₂ ceiling must be written to the destination.

**Is Vietnamese or Sri Lankan desiccated coconut better?**
Neither is universally better. Vietnamese product is competitive on price and proximity with fat around 62 to 66%; Sri Lankan and Indonesian product tends to higher fat (≥ 68%) and a broader certification stack. Choose by your fat target, SO₂ limit and required certifications.

**What moisture and fat content should I specify?**
Moisture ≤ 3% (chips ≤ 2%) and high-fat oil ≥ 60% on a dry basis are standard for quality desiccated coconut. Low fat can indicate immature nuts or adulteration, so specify a minimum, not just a grade.

**What documents prove the specification?**
A certificate of analysis per lot with the test method named, plus traceability to the processing batch and the certifications your market requires (for example HACCP/BRC, and Organic or Halal where relevant).
