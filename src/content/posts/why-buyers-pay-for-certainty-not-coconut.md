Most coconut exporters compete on price and absorb the volatility themselves. The buyers we meet in the EU and US are not, in the end, paying for a lower per-tonne number — they are paying to make price and supply stop moving.

## The real cost is volatility, not the coconut

A procurement team at a European food manufacturer rarely loses sleep over the spot price of desiccated coconut on any single day. What hurts is the *variance* — a contracted volume that arrives short, a price that moves double digits between the quote and the shipment, a supplier who disappears when the harvest tightens. Those costs show up downstream as stockouts, reformulations and renegotiated retail contracts.

So when we talk to buyers, the question is never only "how cheap?" It is "how certain?"

## What certainty actually means

Certainty, in a coconut supply chain, has three parts:

- **Price certainty** — a number that holds from quote to delivery, backed by hedging discipline rather than hope.
- **Supply certainty** — the contracted volume is the delivered volume, because the sourcing zone is locked, not spot-bought.
- **One accountability** — a single counterparty for sourcing, processing, documentation and finance, so there is no gap to fall through.

Each of these is a balance-sheet problem before it is a logistics problem.

## How we underwrite it

We hold the chain and the capital so the buyer does not have to. Grove-to-factory control means we see the harvest before it becomes a number on a contract. Trade finance and credit standing let us carry the working-capital risk that a smallholder network cannot — and price that risk into a firm, L/C-ready offer.

That is the difference between selling a commodity and selling certainty: the first passes volatility down the line; the second absorbs it on purpose, and charges for the service.

## What this means for 2026

Spot prices will keep moving — weather, season and demand will see to that. Our job is to make them stop moving for the buyer. For 2026, that means firmer forward contracts, tighter traceability to the farmer, and a single desk that answers for the whole nut, grove to port.

If you run procurement for a brand that cannot afford a surprise, that is the conversation worth having.
