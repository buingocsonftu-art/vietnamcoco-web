Coconut-shell activated carbon is a high-porosity adsorbent made by carbonizing and steam-activating mature coconut husks. It outperforms coal- and wood-based carbon on hardness, low ash, and micropore volume, which is why water treatment plants, gold-recovery circuits, and gas-purification systems specify it by name. A standard commercial grade carries an iodine number of 1,000 to 1,100 mg/g, ball-pan hardness at or above 98 percent, and ash content under 3 to 5 percent.

## What Is Coconut-Shell Activated Carbon and Where Is It Used?

Coconut-shell activated carbon starts as char from mature shells heated to 600–900 °C in a rotary kiln or multi-hearth furnace. Steam activation then opens the micropore network that gives the material its adsorption capacity. The result is a granular carbon with a predominantly microporous structure, which suits applications that capture small-molecule contaminants rather than large organics.

Water treatment plants use it to remove chlorine, chloramine, taste, and odor compounds at the point of municipal entry and in industrial process water. Gold-recovery operations rely on it in carbon-in-pulp and carbon-in-leach circuits, where its hardness directly affects gold-loss rates through carbon attrition. Air and gas purification systems deploy it to adsorb volatile organic compounds, hydrogen sulfide, and solvent vapors. Each of these markets demands different particle sizes and performance thresholds, which is why understanding the spec sheet matters before you place a purchase order.

## The Specs That Decide Quality

Six measurable parameters determine whether a lot of coconut-shell activated carbon will perform in your process or cause downtime.

**Iodine number** is the headline adsorption metric. It measures micropore volume by reporting milligrams of iodine adsorbed per gram of carbon. For coconut-shell carbon, commercial grades typically range from 1,000 to 1,100 mg/g. Higher iodine numbers signal more available adsorption sites, but they also correlate with slightly lower hardness, so the spec must be balanced against mechanical strength.

**CTC (carbon tetrachloride) activity** reports the weight percentage of CTC vapor the carbon adsorbs under standardized test conditions. Typical coconut-shell grades deliver 50 to 65 percent. This number is the primary screening metric for vapor-phase applications: air-treatment engineers check CTC activity first because it directly reflects the carbon's capacity for organic vapors.

**Ball-pan hardness** measures resistance to abrasion and impact. Coconut-shell carbon is prized for hardness versus coal or wood, routinely testing at or above 98 percent. In gold-recovery CIP/CIL circuits, every percentage-point drop in hardness translates into measurable gold loss through fine carbon reporting to tailings.

**Ash content** for coconut-shell carbon usually falls under 3 to 5 percent, lower than most coal-based carbons. Low ash means fewer inorganic residues leaching into treated water and fewer catalytic hotspots in gas-phase beds. It also reflects cleaner raw material and tighter process control at the activation plant.

**Mesh size** dictates the carbon's hydraulic and aerodynamic behavior. Liquid-phase applications commonly use 8×30 or 12×40 mesh; vapor-phase beds specify 4×8 or 6×12 mesh for lower pressure drop and easier air flow. Mesh size also affects adsorption kinetics: finer particles adsorb faster but raise headloss.

**Apparent density** sits around 0.48 to 0.52 g/cc for standard coconut-shell grades. Density influences how many kilograms fill a given vessel and how the carbon bed fluidizes under backwash or gas surge. It is also a quick sanity check: an abnormally low density can indicate incomplete activation or excessive moisture.

## How Do Application Requirements Dictate the Right Grade?

Water treatment buyers should prioritize iodine number and ash content. An iodine number at or above 1,000 mg/g ensures effective chlorine and chloramine removal across the bed's design life. Ash content under 3 percent keeps dissolved solids low, which matters for boiler-feed and pharmaceutical-grade water. The most common mesh for column service is 12×40, balancing adsorption kinetics against acceptable pressure drop.

Gold-recovery operators need hardness above everything else. Ball-pan hardness at or above 98 percent minimizes carbon fines generation in the aggressive agitation of CIP/CIL tanks. Iodine number is secondary here because the gold-cyanide complex is a relatively large molecule that adsorbs partly on mesopores, not only micropores. Typical mesh for gold circuits is 6×12 or 8×16 to allow slurry flow while keeping the carbon inside interstage screens.

Air and gas purification engineers lead with CTC activity and apparent density. CTC values of 50 to 65 percent confirm adequate micropore volume for solvent-vapor capture. Apparent density near 0.48 to 0.52 g/cc sets the bed weight and contact time. Mesh sizes of 4×8 or 6×12 keep pressure drop manageable in deep beds with high face velocities.

## How Does Raw Shell Quality and Origin Change Performance?

Not all coconut shells produce equivalent activated carbon. Shells from mature, dry-harvested coconuts in coastal Vietnam have a denser cell structure than shells from young or water-logged nuts. Denser precursor char yields a carbon with higher hardness and more uniform micropore development after steam activation. The same kiln conditions applied to lower-grade shells produce carbon with wider pore-size distribution, elevated ash, and inconsistent iodine numbers.

Origin matters for supply consistency. Vietnamese shell supply chains, when managed through direct mill-to-kiln logistics, reduce the variability that comes from stockpiling mixed-origin shells in humid conditions. Moisture swings in stored shells change the carbonization yield and, downstream, the activation uniformity. We track this through [traceability to the processing batch](/sustainability), which lets buyers verify that each shipment comes from controlled shell procurement and a single activation run.

Seasonal harvest patterns also play a role. Peak harvest months produce an abundance of high-density shells; off-season supply may include shells from younger nuts or from regions with different soil mineral content, which raises ash in the finished carbon. Procurement teams that lock in annual volumes with their activated-carbon supplier avoid these seasonal spec drifts.

## Turning Specs Into a Bankable Contract

A purchase specification for coconut-shell activated carbon should list iodine number, CTC activity, ball-pan hardness, ash content, mesh distribution, apparent density, and moisture as numbered clauses with minimum or maximum limits. Each clause should cite the test method—ASTM D4607 for iodine, ASTM D3467 for CTC, ASTM D3802 for hardness—so there is no dispute over which protocol governs the acceptance test.

Define sampling procedure and lot size. A standard practice is one composite sample per 20 metric tons, drawn from at least 10 percent of bags or from a minimum of five points in a bulk shipment. Agree on re-test rights and the rejection protocol before the contract is signed.

Payment terms and logistics round out the contract. For first-time buyers, partial prepayment against shipping documents is common; repeat orders often shift to a letter of credit or open-account terms once supply consistency is established. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**What iodine number should I specify for potable water treatment?**
For municipal water treatment, specify an iodine number of at least 1,000 mg/g tested per ASTM D4607. This level of micropore volume provides effective chlorine and chloramine removal over a typical 12-month bed life. If your feed water has high organic loading (TOC above 4 mg/L), consider requesting 1,050 mg/g or higher to extend run length.

**Why is coconut-shell carbon preferred over coal-based carbon for gold recovery?**
Ball-pan hardness is the deciding factor. Coconut-shell carbon routinely tests at or above 98 percent hardness, while coal-based grades typically reach 90 to 95 percent. In CIP/CIL circuits, lower hardness means higher carbon attrition rates, which directly increases gold losses in tailings. The lower ash content of coconut-shell carbon also reduces silica scaling on carbon surfaces.

**What mesh size works best for vapor-phase air treatment?**
Most air and gas purification systems use 4×8 or 6×12 mesh. These coarser particle sizes produce a lower pressure drop across deep beds and resist fluidization under high face velocities. Finer meshes such as 8×30 can increase adsorption kinetics but may exceed the blower capacity of an existing system.

**How does apparent density affect my vessel loading?**
Apparent density, typically 0.48 to 0.52 g/cc for coconut-shell carbon, determines how many kilograms of carbon fit into a given vessel volume. A lower density means lighter loading per cubic meter, which can reduce adsorption capacity per cycle if contact time is not recalculated. Always specify a density range in your purchase order and verify it against the supplier's lot certificate.

**Can I lock in specs across multiple shipments?**
Yes, but only if the contract specifies test methods, acceptance limits, and sampling protocols for every lot. Pair this with a supplier that maintains traceability to each processing batch, so seasonal or raw-material variation does not silently change the product you receive. Annual volume commitments also give the supplier incentive to reserve consistent shell stock.
